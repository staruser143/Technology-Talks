**Service Rationalization** is the systematic evaluation and optimization of an organization’s existing applications, systems, or services to determine **which should be kept, modernized, consolidated, replaced, or retired**—based on business value, cost, risk, and strategic alignment.

In simple terms:  
👉 *“Do we still need this service, and if yes, in what form?”*

***

## Why Service Rationalization Is Done

Organizations typically do service rationalization to:

*   Reduce **cost and redundancy**
*   Simplify **IT portfolios**
*   Improve **agility and scalability**
*   Prepare for **cloud migration or modernization**
*   Reduce **technical debt and operational risk**
*   Align IT investments with **business strategy**

***

## What Gets Rationalized

Service rationalization can apply to:

*   Business applications (custom apps, COTS, legacy systems)
*   Shared services and APIs
*   Microservices in a distributed architecture
*   Infrastructure-dependent services
*   Data platforms and integration services

***

## Core Dimensions Evaluated

Each service is assessed across common dimensions:

### 1. **Business Value**

*   Does it support a critical business capability?
*   Is it revenue-generating, regulatory, or differentiating?

### 2. **Technical Health**

*   Age of the technology stack
*   Maintainability and scalability
*   Security and compliance risks

### 3. **Cost**

*   License costs
*   Infrastructure and support costs
*   Operational overhead

### 4. **Usage & Redundancy**

*   Active usage vs shelfware
*   Overlap with other services

### 5. **Strategic Fit**

*   Alignment with target architecture
*   Cloud / API / microservices readiness

***

## Typical Rationalization Outcomes (6R-style)

While exact labels vary, outcomes commonly fall into these categories:

*   **Retain** – Keep as-is (still valuable and healthy)
*   **Rehost** – Move with minimal changes (e.g., lift-and-shift)
*   **Refactor** – Improve architecture or code
*   **Re-platform** – Move to managed platforms with some changes
*   **Replace** – Swap with a SaaS or COTS solution
*   **Retire** – Decommission entirely

***

## Process at a High Level

1.  **Inventory** all services/applications
2.  **Assess** across agreed criteria
3.  **Score and categorize**
4.  **Decide target action**
5.  **Create a phased roadmap**
6.  **Execute with governance**

***

## Concrete Example

Assume a healthcare payer company:

*   5 different **enrollment services** across LOBs
*   3 of them perform similar validation logic
*   1 is tightly coupled to legacy mainframe

After service rationalization:

*   ✅ 1 core enrollment service is **retained and modernized**
*   🔁 2 services are **merged**
*   ☁️ 1 is **replaced** by a SaaS eligibility engine
*   🗑️ 1 unused service is **retired**

Result: lower cost, simpler architecture, faster onboarding.

***

## How It Differs from Nearby Concepts

*   **Service Rationalization** → decision-making and categorization
*   **Modernization** → implementation of selected changes
*   **Portfolio Management** → ongoing governance and investment tracking

Rationalization comes **before** transformation.

***

## When It Is Most Critical

*   Before **cloud migration**
*   During **M\&A integration**
*   When facing **runaway IT costs**
*   Prior to introducing **agentic / GenAI layers**
*   While moving from monoliths to **domain-aligned services**

***

## One-Line Summary

> **Service rationalization is the structured process of deciding which services to keep, change, consolidate, or retire to maximize business value and minimize complexity.**
